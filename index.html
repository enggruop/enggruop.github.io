Publications

A Comparison of Korean and EU Competition Law on the Regulation of Information Exchange
An, H., Chon, H., & Kim, J. Y. · The Korean Journal of Industrial Organization (KJIO)
Abstract

The term “stand-alone information exchange” is used to describe an information exchange that is treated as a cartel in its own right. Stand-alone information exchange is distinguishable from traditional cartels in that it may take various forms and, without leaving evidence of an agreement, can generate anti-competitive effects comparable to collusion. Moreover, it may distort the competitive structure before market outcomes fully materialize, thereby producing restrictive effects either on its own or through subsequent conduct. This study structures and compares the Korean and EU competition law regimes that subsume stand-alone information exchange as an unlawful concerted practice along three dimensions: undertakings, coordination and anti-competitive effects of that coordination. In Korea, stand-alone information exchange is regulated within a prescriptive rule-based regulation that confines enforcement to enumerated prohibited categories, requiring an agreement to establish coordination and assessing the anti-competitive effects largely by reference to observed market outcomes. However, this framework fails to take adequate account of the characteristics of information exchange and appears inconsistent with the legislative intent to sanction information exchange as an independent infringement. By contrast, in the EU, information exchange is regulated through a principles-based regulation, the notion of “concerted practice” that is not constrained by the need to prove an agreement, and an assessment of anti-competitive effects that considers the nature and purpose of the information exchanged, enabling more effective control of such conduct. On this basis, this paper evaluates the Korean regulation of information exchange in light of the EU competition law.

Simulating Analyst Forecast Behavior using LLMs working paper
An, H., & Lee, S. · SSRN · Replication Package
Abstract

We examine whether a commercial large language model (LLM) can match human analysts in numerical earnings per share (EPS) forecasting. Using 121,369 forecasts for 607 Korean listed firms over 2018–2025, we generate paired LLM forecasts utilizing only information publicly available as of the analyst forecast date, with external search disabled. Human analysts remain more accurate on average: median forecast errors are 1.9% of stock price for analysts and 2.3% for the LLM, implying that the LLM underperforms analysts by about 20%. The LLM outperforms consensus forecasts but remains less accurate than analysts’ prior forecasts. The analyst advantage is larger for loss-making, highly leveraged, and smaller firms, as well as for firms with greater analyst disagreement. Comparing model generations under identical prompts, GPT-5.4-nano closes 67.5% of the gap between GPT-4.1-nano and human analysts. Overall, current commercial LLMs do not yet perform on par with human analysts, but their relative performance improves across model generations and is strongest in settings with richer public information and lower uncertainty.
